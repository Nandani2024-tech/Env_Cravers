Clinical Triage Assistant — OpenEnv Environment
________________


1. What We're Building
An AI training and evaluation environment where agents learn to make emergency room triage decisions — the same decisions real triage nurses make every day under pressure.
The agent sees patients arriving with symptoms, vitals, and medical history. It must decide who gets treated first, who can wait, and when resources are scarce — who gets the doctor and who gets the bed.
This is not a toy problem. Every hospital in the world does this. Getting it wrong costs lives.
________________


2. Why This Problem
Every emergency room in the world faces the same crisis — too many patients, not enough resources, decisions made in seconds.
Currently triage is done by experienced nurses following protocols like the Manchester Triage System and ESI (Emergency Severity Index). These are well-documented, rule-based systems — which means:
* We have ground truth to build graders from
* The rules are deterministic and fair
* The domain is universally understood
* The stakes are obviously real
An AI agent trained in this environment could:
* Assist triage nurses in high-pressure situations
* Help train medical students
* Power simulation tools for hospital management
* Be evaluated for deployment in resource-limited healthcare settings

4. The Three Tasks
Task 1 — Single Patient Classification (Easy)
What the agent sees: One patient. Their age, symptoms, vitals (heart rate, blood pressure, temperature), and a brief description of their complaint.
What the agent must do: Classify the patient into one of three urgency levels:
* 🔴 Critical — needs immediate attention
* 🟡 Moderate — can wait 30 minutes
* 🟢 Low — can wait 2+ hours
How it's graded: Compared against Manchester Triage System rules. Exact match = full score. Adjacent level = partial score. Opposite extreme = near zero.
Why it's easy: One patient, one decision, clear rules.
________________


Task 2 — Queue Reprioritization (Medium)
What the agent sees: A waiting room of 10 patients, each with their initial triage level. Midway through, new information arrives — vitals change, new symptoms emerge, a patient deteriorates.
What the agent must do: Continuously reprioritize the queue as information updates. Decide who moves up, who stays, who can wait longer.
How it's graded: Scored on how closely the final queue ordering matches the medically correct ordering. Partial credit for each correctly positioned patient.
Why it's medium: Multiple patients, dynamic information, ordering matters not just classification.
________________


Task 3 — Resource Allocation Under Constraints (Hard)
What the agent sees: 15 patients in various states. Available resources: 2 doctors, 3 beds, 1 trauma bay. Each resource has different capability — not every patient can go to every resource.
What the agent must do: Assign patients to resources optimally. Decide who gets the trauma bay, who gets a doctor, who gets a bed, and who must wait. As time passes, patients' conditions change and the agent must re-allocate.
How it's graded: Multi-factor score — correct resource matching, minimized wait time for critical patients, zero preventable deteriorations, efficient use of available resources.
Why it's hard: Constrained optimization + time pressure + changing state + multiple competing priorities. Even frontier models struggle with this.
________________


5. Reward Function Design
The reward is never binary. Every single step gives the agent a signal.
What gets rewarded:
* Correctly classifying urgency → positive reward proportional to confidence of match
* Moving a deteriorating patient up the queue quickly → bonus
* Efficiently using resources without leaving critical patients waiting → bonus
* Completing the episode without any preventable deteriorations → completion bonus
What gets penalized:
* Classifying a critical patient as low urgency → heavy penalty
* Assigning a patient to an inappropriate resource → penalty
* Repeating the same action with no new information → penalty (prevents loops)
* Taking no action while a critical patient waits → time-decay penalty
The key design principle: A nurse who gets 8/10 patients right should score better than one who gets 5/10. The reward reflects real partial progress at every step.

6. Environment Design (Non-Technical Overview)
What the agent sees (Observation):
* Patient details: age, gender, chief complaint, symptoms list
* Vitals: heart rate, blood pressure, temperature, oxygen saturation
* Current queue state: who is waiting, how long they've waited
* Available resources: what's free, what's occupied
* Time elapsed in episode
What the agent can do (Action):
* Assign a urgency level to a patient
* Move a patient's position in the queue
* Assign a patient to a specific resource
* Request more information about a patient
* Mark a patient as stable/discharged
Episode boundaries:
* Each episode = one ER shift scenario
* Episode ends when all patients are classified/assigned OR maximum steps reached
* Clean reset between episodes — fresh patient set, fresh resources

Dependency Flow
1. Level 0 (Foundation): config.py, seeds.py, constants/*.py. These have no internal dependencies and define the world rules.
2. Level 1 (Data & Models): models/*.py, data/patient_factory.py. These consume Level 0 to define the data structures.
3. Level 2 (Atomic Engines): vitals/, resources/, info/, queue/, data/scenario_builder.py. These consume Level 1 to provide functional logic (e.g., patient deterioration, info unlocking).
4. Level 3 (Management Layer): state/, validator.py, termination.py. These monitor the atomic engines and enforce simulation boundaries.
5. Level 4 (Task & Reward): tasks/, reward_engine.py, graders/. These orchestrate the management layer into specific scenarios and calculate performance.
6. Level 5 (Interface): environment.py (OpenEnv). This consumes everything to provide a unified reset/step/state interface.
7. Level 6 (API): api/. This wraps the OpenEnv interface in a FastAPI server.
8. Level 7 (External): inference.py, Dockerfile. These interact with the running API or build the system.
